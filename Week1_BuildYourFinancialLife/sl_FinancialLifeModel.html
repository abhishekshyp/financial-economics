How to think about personal finance04

Gross income = Taxes + Consumption + Savings

"Every rupee you earn has only three possible destinations."

Before you even decide, the government takes a share as taxes — and returns public services for it. That portion is simply not available for personal use. What's left splits between consumption (spent now) and savings (kept for later).

The point of it all05

Why do we earn money?

To consume. Food, housing, clothes, education, entertainment, travel — the things a life is actually made of.

Economics says consumption generates utility — happiness, or satisfaction. So money is not the goal; it is a claim on things you value. In a very real sense, money has value only when it is converted into consumption.

A rupee never spent, and never meant to be, was never really wealth.
Reframing the third bucket06

Savings are simply future consumption.

"I choose to consume later instead of today."

You receive one whole pizza each month. One slice goes to the government. Several slices feed today's you. The rest are frozen — put away for a future, hungrier you.

Saving isn't the opposite of spending. It's spending scheduled for later.
Your monthly pizza, sliced
Same defaults as the identity — 15% claimed, 59.5% eaten now, 25.5% frozen.
Where this course goes07

The rest of this course is about making that third bucket — your savings — work as efficiently as possible, through investing.

Frozen slices don't have to sit idle. Invested well, deferred consumption grows — so tomorrow's you can eat far more than you gave up today. That growth is the subject of everything ahead.

The answer09

After 30 days, the penny is worth over $5.3 million.

For 27 days it looks like nothing — a few thousand dollars against a certain million. Then exponential growth arrives all at once: it only overtakes $1M on day 28, and finishes at $5,368,709.

The lesson isn't the penny. It's that compounding hides its power until the very end.

Run the machine in reverse11

Present Value — now reverse the story of time.

"Suppose I promise to give you ₹1 crore."

Would you still accept it if I only pay you after 100 years? Almost no one would. Why? Because money loses value with time — future money simply isn't worth today's money.

A promise far enough away is barely a gift at all.

One idea, two directions13

Finance has two time machines.

→→→

Compounding

Moves money forward in time. A rupee today becomes many rupees later — the future-value engine.

←←←

Discounting

Brings money backward in time. A rupee promised later is worth less today — the present-value engine.

Same physics, opposite gears: (1+r)n forward, 1/(1+r)n backward.

The hidden asset15

Person A may be worth far more — they own 40 years of future salary that simply hasn't arrived yet.

Your degree, skills, experience, health and network aren't just personal qualities. They generate future cash flows — a stream of paychecks stretching decades ahead. Discount that stream to today and you get a number, often a very large one.

This present value of your future earnings is your Human Capital.

Converting one into the other17

When young, most of your wealth is invisible.

It lives as the ability to earn. As you age, you convert human capital into financial capital — paycheck by paycheck, saving by saving. The point where they cross is the central event of a saver's life.

"Early on, your biggest asset is your future paycheck. Later, it should be your portfolio. The journey is converting one into the other."

Theory · Modigliani–Brumberg (1954)18

The Life-Cycle Hypothesis

Income arrives as a hump — low when young, peaking mid-career, then zero at retirement. But you want to consume smoothly your whole life. So you build a wealth hump: save on the way up, draw it down on the way out.

Save while you earn, so you can spend when you can't. Wealth is a bridge across the income gap.
Income humps; consumption stays level; wealth bridges the gap
Stylized (returns set aside to show the pure hump). Income ends at 60; the green wealth hump is built, then drawn down to a small bequest.
Theory · Friedman (1957)19

The Permanent Income Hypothesis

People spend based on their lifetime resources, not this month's paycheck. A one-off windfall barely moves consumption — you spread it across all remaining years. Try it: drop a ₹5 L bonus into year 10 and watch consumption hardly flinch.

Transitory income is saved; only permanent income is consumed.
Bumpy income vs. smooth consumption
Income jumps around; consumption tracks permanent income — a near-flat line.
Read the model critically20

The model is deliberately simple. Interrogating it is the point.

LIMIT 01

No uncertainty

Returns, income and lifespan are fixed. So there is no sequence-of-returns risk, no ruin probability, no longevity risk. The corpus is one path — not a distribution.

LIMIT 02

One constant return

A single fixed return ignores the age-based glide path — high equity when young, de-risking with age — that human-capital theory actually implies.

LIMIT 03

Smooth, exogenous spending

Real lives are lumpy — education, marriage, housing, vehicles, emergencies — and often debt-financed. The model has one asset, no housing, no explicit liabilities.
